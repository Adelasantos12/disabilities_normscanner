# Switzerland Legal System Module
## Country Reference Framework for NormTrace

This document provides the country-specific legal reference framework used by NormTrace for context-sensitive normative analysis in Switzerland.

Its purpose is to provide the constitutional, institutional, and legislative context required to interpret domestic legal instruments in light of international standards, specifically focusing on the monist system and federal structure of Switzerland.

## 1. Monist system and international law
Switzerland follows a **monist** legal tradition. This means that international law forms part of the domestic legal framework from the moment it enters into force for Switzerland, without the need for a separate act of transformation or incorporation into national law.

### Relevance for rights analysis:
- The Confederation and the cantons are bound to respect international law.
- However, for an international provision to be invoked directly before a Swiss court, it must be determined whether it is **directly applicable (self-executing)** or if it requires further domestic legislation to be operational.
- In principle, international law prevails over conflicting domestic law, with limited doctrinal exceptions such as the Schubert practice (where the Federal Assembly deliberately and consciously legislates contrary to an international treaty, though this exception is generally not applied to human rights treaties).

## 2. The CRPD in Switzerland
- **Ratification:** Switzerland acceded to the Convention on the Rights of Persons with Disabilities (CRPD) on 15 April 2014.
- **Optional Protocol:** Switzerland has *not* ratified the Optional Protocol to the CRPD.

### Analytical relevance for NormTrace:
When assessing normative alignment in Switzerland, analysis must consider the interplay between the CRPD and existing Swiss legislation, notably the Federal Act on the Elimination of Inequalities for Persons with Disabilities (BehiG / LHand / LDis = SR 151.3). The analysis must evaluate whether the domestic legal instrument is compatible with the CRPD, recognising the ongoing debate regarding the direct applicability of specific CRPD provisions in Swiss courts.

## 3. Federal structure and competence distribution
Switzerland is a federal state consisting of the Confederation, 26 cantons, and municipalities.

- The **Confederation** has only those competences expressly delegated to it by the Federal Constitution.
- The **cantons** are sovereign except where their sovereignty is limited by the Federal Constitution. They exercise all rights that are not transferred to the Confederation.

### Analytical relevance for NormTrace:
- When reviewing a domestic legal instrument, it is crucial to verify whether the subject matter falls under federal or cantonal competence.
- In areas like education or social assistance, cantons play a primary role, which can lead to significant implementation variability across the country.
- The analysis should reflect this division of competences and not assume uniform nationwide implementation of federal laws or international standards where cantonal autonomy is paramount.

## 4. Legal compatibility and rights review
Unlike systems with a centralised constitutional court that can strike down federal laws, the Swiss Federal Supreme Court cannot invalidate a federal act for unconstitutionality (Article 190 of the Federal Constitution).

However, because international law is binding, the Court can review federal laws for compatibility with international human rights treaties (such as the ECHR).

### Analytical relevance for NormTrace:
- Conventionality analysis in Switzerland is framed as an international human rights compatibility analysis or treaty-compatibility analysis.
- Arguments regarding legal compatibility must navigate the specific boundaries of judicial review in Switzerland, particularly the relationship between federal acts and international human rights obligations.
