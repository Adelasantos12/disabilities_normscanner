# Legal Argumentation Framework

This framework provides a general, portable methodology for translating analytical findings into structured legal arguments suited to different forums across jurisdictions (e.g., Mexico, Switzerland).

## 1. Required components of each argument
Every structured argument must clearly identify:
- **Core finding:** The legal problem, omission, or incompatibility.
- **Domestic legal instrument:** The specific provision under review.
- **International standard engaged:** The applicable treaty provision or interpretive material.
- **Type of legal deficiency:** The nature of the gap (e.g., structural exclusion, regression, implementation-oriented gap).
- **Relevant doctrinal or interpretive support:** Citations to General Comments, case-law, or constitutional clauses.
- **Proposed line of reform or remedy:** A potential reform pathway, litigation strategy, or advocacy position.

## 2. Argument organisation and prioritisation
Arguments should be structured and prioritised based on:
- **Urgency:** Does the deficiency violate an absolute right or a right subject to progressive realisation?
- **Remediability:** Can it be fixed through administrative regulation, or does it require constitutional or statutory reform?
- **Litigation potential:** Is the issue ripe for judicial review under domestic mechanisms?
- **Institutional forum:** Which body is best positioned to address the gap?
- **Expected effect on rights-holders:** What is the practical impact of the proposed remedy?

## 3. Forum differentiation
Adapt the structure and tone of the argument to the intended audience:
- **Strategic litigation / domestic judicial review:** Focus on binding precedent, constitutional parameters, direct applicability, and remedies.
- **UN or international mechanisms:** Focus on state obligations, concluding observations, and systemic patterns of non-compliance.
- **Legislative advocacy:** Focus on policy coherence, budget implications, and drafting reforms.
- **Public advocacy:** Focus on lived experience, accessibility of language, and broad rights narratives.

## 4. Methodological caution
- Do not produce vague or purely rhetorical conclusions.
- Distinguish diagnosis from recommendation.
- Avoid overstating legal certainty where interpretive controversies exist.
- Ensure the argument specifies the level of normativity at which reform is required.
