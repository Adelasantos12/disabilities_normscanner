# Structural Analysis Framework

This framework provides a general methodology for dissecting the formal anatomy, institutional architecture, and enforceability structure of any domestic legal instrument.

It is intended to be applied across jurisdictions (e.g., Mexico, Switzerland) to assess whether rights are structurally enforceable or primarily declaratory.

## 1. Formal anatomy
Identify the formal characteristics of the domestic legal instrument:
- Type of instrument (e.g., constitution, federal law, cantonal law, regulation).
- Place in the domestic normative hierarchy (consult the relevant country module).
- Date of enactment and latest reform.
- Structural organisation (titles, chapters, sections, articles).

## 2. Normative actor mapping
Identify the actors assigned responsibilities, powers, or roles by the instrument:
- Executive, legislative, and judicial authorities.
- Autonomous or decentralised institutions.
- Subnational authorities (e.g., states, cantons, municipalities).
- Private sector actors and civil society organisations.
- Rights-holders themselves.

For each identified actor, map:
- Duties and obligations.
- Powers and faculties.
- Role in implementation and monitoring.
- Mechanisms of accountability and enforceability.

## 3. Responsibility flow
Assess how the domestic legal instrument distributes responsibility:
- Does it centralise power, delegate downward, or disperse duties without coordination?
- Are concurrent obligations clearly articulated?
- Is there a coordinating body with legal authority, budgetary support, and sanctioning capacity?

## 4. Enforceability mechanisms
Evaluate the practical mechanisms that give effect to the rights:
- Sanctions for non-compliance.
- Judicial or administrative remedies.
- Independent monitoring mechanisms.
- Measurable targets, reporting duties, and implementation deadlines.
- Transitional provisions that move the norm beyond mere declaration.
