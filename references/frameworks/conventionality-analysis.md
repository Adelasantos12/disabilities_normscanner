# Conventionality Analysis Framework

This framework provides a structured methodology for assessing the legal compatibility between a domestic legal instrument and the applicable international standard.

It focuses on legal function and effect rather than textual symmetry alone, making it applicable to different constitutional and international regimes (e.g., Mexico, Switzerland).

## 1. Applicable standard
Identify the applicable international and domestic interpretive framework:
- Treaty provisions.
- Constitutional clauses (consult country modules).
- General Comments and concluding observations.
- Regional human rights standards (e.g., ECHR, Inter-American Court).
- Domestic high-court doctrine.

**Note on Jurisdictional Distinction:**
- **Mexico:** Conventionality control within the constitutional parameter of rights review.
- **Switzerland:** International human rights compatibility analysis / treaty-compatibility analysis within a monist system.

Do not assume the Mexican model of conventionality control applies unchanged to Switzerland.

## 2. Functional contrast
Assess whether the domestic legal instrument is consistent with principles such as:
- Non-regression.
- Progressivity.
- Maximum protection (pro persona).
- Effectiveness.
- Equality and non-discrimination.
- Accessibility, participation, and autonomy.

## 3. Gap identification
Classify findings where relevant as:
- Total absence of the standard.
- Weak or declaratory recognition without enforceability.
- Incompatibility with the international standard.
- Regression from previously recognized rights.
- Indirect or structural discrimination.
- Omission affecting a specific subgroup of rights-holders.
- Implementation-oriented gap (e.g., lack of budget, monitoring, or sanctions).

The objective is to assess the legal significance of the difference between the domestic norm and the international standard.
