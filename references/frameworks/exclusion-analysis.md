# Exclusion Analysis Framework

This framework provides a general methodology for identifying omissions, exclusions, and subgroup-specific barriers within any domestic legal instrument.

It is designed to assess what the legal text does *not* adequately articulate and the differentiated effects of that absence, applying a portable analytical lens across jurisdictions.

## 1. Subject of rights
Assess how the legal subject is defined:
- Who is explicitly recognised as a rights-holder?
- Is the definition restrictive, expansive, or ambiguous?
- Are subgroups omitted implicitly?
- Does the instrument adopt a rights-based construction or an assistentialist/medicalised model?

## 2. Gender perspective
Review the domestic legal instrument for gendered impacts:
- Does it recognise gendered forms of disadvantage?
- Does it address women and girls specifically where relevant?
- Does it account for gender-based violence?
- Does it avoid exclusionary generic language?
- Does it anticipate differentiated legal effects between genders?

## 3. Intersectionality
Examine whether the instrument accounts for intersecting forms of structural disadvantage:
- Indigeneity.
- Migration status.
- Age (children, youth, older persons).
- Poverty or socioeconomic deprivation.
- Rurality.
- Sexual orientation and gender identity.
- Deprivation of liberty.

## 4. Indigenous perspective and cultural accessibility
Where relevant to the jurisdiction, assess whether the domestic legal instrument:
- Recognises indigenous peoples and internal normative systems.
- Incorporates free, prior and informed consultation standards.
- Accounts for cultural, linguistic, geographic, and documentary accessibility.
- Avoids assuming a single homogeneous rights environment.

## 5. Language as exclusion
Analyse the legal terminology used:
- Does it reflect outdated, pejorative, or medicalising assumptions?
- Does it narrow the legal subject through paternalistic language?
- Does it frame persons as passive beneficiaries rather than active rights-holders?
- Does the language reproduce subordinating assumptions?

## 6. Normative silence
Identify how exclusion arises through omission:
- Absolute or relative omission.
- Declaratory recognition of rights without a corresponding enforcement mechanism.
- Budgetary silence or under-specification.
- Delegation to future regulation without a clear deadline.
