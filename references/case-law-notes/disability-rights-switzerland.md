# Case-Law Notes
## Disability Rights and Conventionality Analysis in Switzerland

This file provides selected case-law notes relevant to disability-rights analysis, conventionality analysis (understood in Switzerland as treaty-compatibility analysis), and the domestic implementation of international human rights standards within the Swiss legal framework.

It is intended as a support document for NormTrace. It does not replace full doctrinal case analysis. Its purpose is to identify decisions and interpretive materials that may be relevant when assessing **legal compatibility**, institutional design, equality, legal capacity, accessibility, and **implementation-oriented gaps** within the Swiss domestic legal framework.

---

## 1. Switzerland: Federal Supreme Court (Bundesgericht / Tribunal Fédéral / Tribunale Federale)

The Swiss Federal Supreme Court plays a crucial role in interpreting domestic law in light of international human rights obligations, particularly the European Convention on Human Rights (ECHR) and UN human rights treaties.

### Analytical significance
While Switzerland does not have a constitutional court that can invalidate federal laws (Article 190 of the Federal Constitution), the Federal Supreme Court is bound to apply international law. In cases of conflict between a federal act and international human rights law, the Court generally gives precedence to international law (the "Schubert practice" exception is rarely applied to human rights treaties).

### Relevance for NormTrace
When assessing the Swiss domestic legal framework against the CRPD, analysis must consider the interplay between the treaty provisions and existing Swiss constitutional guarantees (e.g., Article 8(2) prohibiting discrimination, Article 8(4) on eliminating inequalities affecting persons with disabilities).

## 2. The European Court of Human Rights (ECtHR)

Switzerland is a party to the ECHR, and judgments of the ECtHR are binding on Switzerland when it is a party to the case. Even when not directly involved, the ECtHR's jurisprudence significantly influences the interpretation of fundamental rights in Switzerland.

### Relevance for NormTrace
While the CRPD is the primary international standard for disability rights, the ECtHR has increasingly integrated CRPD principles into its interpretation of the ECHR (e.g., in cases concerning legal capacity, involuntary treatment, or inclusive education).

When analysing Swiss domestic legal instruments, identify ECHR-relevant pathways that complement or reinforce CRPD standards.

## 3. UN treaty body interpretive materials

Interpretive materials from UN treaty bodies (such as General Comments and concluding observations) are persuasive in Switzerland, though they lack formally binding force.

### Relevance for NormTrace
Use CRPD Committee General Comments (e.g., on Article 12 - Equal recognition before the law, or Article 24 - Education) to clarify the **international standard** against which Swiss cantonal or federal laws are assessed.

## 4. Methodological note for analysts

These case-law notes should be used selectively and analytically.

They are not intended as a checklist of authorities to cite automatically. Their purpose is to help identify:
- doctrinally relevant standards;
- interpretive developments in disability rights;
- ECHR and Swiss constitutional principles relevant to domestic legal analysis.

In NormTrace, precedents and interpretive materials should be used to clarify:
- the **international standard**;
- the **domestic legal problem**;
- and the **type of normative gap** or **implementation-oriented gap** under review.

Avoid overclaiming direct domestic CRPD jurisprudence where not clearly mapped, as the direct applicability of many CRPD provisions remains a subject of doctrinal debate in Switzerland. Always frame the analysis cautiously, using the CRPD and Swiss constitutional guarantees together.
